QuickBooks Desktop Is Ending May 31. Here's What Every Wine & Spirits Distributor Needs to Know.

Intuit is pulling the plug on QuickBooks Desktop 2023 on May 31, 2026. If you're one of the estimated 70 to 120 wine and spirits distributors in the $1–20M range still running it, you've got about two months to figure out what comes next.

This isn't a soft nudge. After May 31, payroll processing stops. Bank feeds disconnect. Payment integrations go dark. Your accounting system doesn't just get old — it stops working.

And before you assume the answer is "just move to QuickBooks Online," let me save you six months of pain.

QuickBooks Online Doesn't Know Your Business

Here's the thing Intuit won't tell you on the migration page: QuickBooks Online was built for service businesses, freelancers, and retail shops. It was not built for three-tier distribution.

That matters because your business runs on things QBO has never heard of:

Excise tax. Federal excise tax on spirits runs $13.50 per proof gallon. Wine ranges from $1.07 to $3.40 per gallon depending on alcohol content. Beer is $18 per barrel (or $3.50 if you're under 2 million barrels). QBO has a sales tax module. It does not have an excise tax module. These are fundamentally different calculations, and getting them wrong means you're either overpaying or getting audited.

Three-tier pricing. Your margins flow through three tiers: supplier to distributor, distributor to retailer, retailer to consumer. Each tier takes roughly 25–30%. You need to track FOB pricing, depletion allowances, promotional pricing, and volume discounts — often across multiple states with different rules. QBO gives you "products and services" with a single price field.

Commission tracking. Your sales reps work on commission structures that vary by account, by brand, by volume tier, and sometimes by month. Some distributors also pay broker commissions on top of that. In QBO, you're back to a spreadsheet.

Depletion reporting. Every supplier you carry wants to know how their brands are moving. Depletion reports — cases sold by account, by SKU, by time period — are table stakes in this industry. QBO doesn't have a concept of depletions. It has invoices.

State-specific compliance. If you operate in a control state like Virginia or Pennsylvania, the rules are different again. PALCB requires specific ordering systems. Control state pricing follows state-mandated structures. QBO doesn't distinguish between a free market and a control market.

The Real Cost of Migrating to the Wrong System

I've talked to dozens of distributors who tried to make QBO work. The pattern is always the same:

They migrate. They discover QBO can't handle their pricing or reporting. They start building workarounds in Excel. Within three months, they're running QBO plus four spreadsheets plus a whiteboard — which is worse than what they had before, and now they're paying $80/month for the privilege.

The hidden cost isn't the subscription. It's the staff time. An office manager spending 10 hours a week on manual workarounds at $25/hour is $13,000 a year in labor just to compensate for software that doesn't fit.

Meanwhile, one pricing error on a 500-case order at $2 per case off is a $1,000 mistake. Multiply that across a month of orders and you're bleeding margin without knowing it.

What to Do Instead

The wine and spirits distribution industry has purpose-built tools now. Five years ago, your only options were VIP Price 2.0 (expensive, complex, built for the biggest players) or spreadsheets. That's changed.

Modern industry-specific platforms handle excise tax calculations natively. They understand three-tier pricing and can run margin scenarios — what we call "gaming models" — both forward (from COGS to SRP) and backward (from SRP to FOB). They track commissions, generate depletion reports, and handle state-specific compliance without bolted-on workarounds.

The right tool should cost less than the labor you're currently spending on manual processes. If you're paying someone to re-key orders, reconcile pricing spreadsheets, or manually calculate commissions, that's your budget for something better.

Your Pre-May 31 Checklist

Don't wait until May 30. Here's what to do now:

  • 1. Export everything. Pull your chart of accounts, vendor list, customer list, open invoices, and transaction history from QB Desktop. Do this while it still works. Export to CSV — not QBB backup files that only open in QuickBooks.
  • 2. Audit your workarounds. List every spreadsheet, every manual process, every "we just do it this way" workflow that lives outside QuickBooks. This is your real requirements list. Any new system needs to handle these natively, not recreate them.
  • 3. Map your pricing structure. Document your FOB prices, margin targets, depletion allowances, and commission structures by brand and by state. This is the data any new system needs on day one.
  • 4. Talk to your suppliers. Ask what reporting they need from you. Depletion reports, pricing compliance, promotional tracking — get the actual formats. Your new system should generate these automatically, not require you to build them from scratch.
  • 5. Evaluate industry-specific options before generic ones. Talk to vendors who understand the three-tier system, excise tax, and state-by-state compliance. Ask them to show you commission tracking, depletion reporting, and multi-state pricing in their actual product — not a slide deck. If they can't demo it live, they don't have it.

The Bottom Line

QuickBooks Desktop served this industry well for 20 years. But it's ending, and the replacement Intuit is pushing doesn't fit your business. You've got a narrow window to make a smart decision.

The worst outcome isn't staying on QB Desktop too long. It's rushing into QBO because it feels familiar, then spending the next year building spreadsheet workarounds that cost more than the right tool would have.

Talk to someone who understands wine and spirits distribution before you migrate to the wrong thing. Your margins — and your staff's sanity — will thank you.
